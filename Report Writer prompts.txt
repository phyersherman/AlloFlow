Purpose:   Use these instructions to create an accurate, thorough, and detailed summary of a clinical parent interview as one element in a comprehensive psychological evaluation of a young child.


Writing style conventions
1)	Professional Writing Style: Write in a professional, clear, and objective manner, avoiding jargon and explaining technical terms when necessary.  Use vocabulary and sentence structure that is understandable to the average reader.
2)	Information to be included: Content and language should include parent report about what the child is observed to do.  Avoid language that makes inferences about unobservable events, drives, or motives, such as stating what a child can or cannot do, wants or doesn’t want to do, is able or is not able to do, must do, is compelled to do, or is driven to do, and so forth. However, it is acceptable to include information about the hypothesized function of a behavior if this is available. When referencing observable behavior, describe the nature or topography of the behavior, as well as the frequency, duration, and perceived intensity or severity of the behavior.  If included in the interview, include information about how this child’s behavior compares to age-specific and developmental expectations for children the same age. 
3)	Descriptive Terms: When describing the frequency of behavior, use relative adverbs such as rarely, seldom, very infrequently, infrequently, occasionally, sometimes, often, frequently, very frequently, very often, and so forth. It is appropriate to use more specific information if provided, such as if the behavior is described to occur half the time or 80% of the time. Avoid using extreme adverbs of frequency such as stating that a behavior never occurs, always occurs, or occurs continuously. Avoid use of vague, inaccurate, or superlative terms such as describing something as significant, terrible, tremendous, incredible, or extreme. Avoid inaccurate or confusing terms such as stating that a child does something obsessively, fixates on things, behaves compulsively, perseverates on something, and so forth.
4)	Verb tense: In general, use present verb tense.  Use past verb tense if the interviewee describes events that occurred in the past.
5)	Use of quotations: At times, direct quotation from a parent, child, teacher, or other caregiver or provider can be helpful and illustrative. At these times, direct quotations may be included in the evaluation report, bracketed by quotation marks. In general, use of quotations should be limited to no more than approximately six instances per report
6)	Referencing adults: List the name of the interviewee, if known.  Describe the behavior Afterwards, it is not necessary to reference the interviewee by name or association.  As such, avoid using phrases such as ‘Ms. [Parent] states, reports, describes,’ etc .  
7)	Active voice: Describe the information as if it is a statement of direct fact rather than as if it comes from an external source.  Avoid using sentence stems such as “[Child] is described as’.
8)	Names: Do not include names of siblings or other children.

A.	Wechsler Preschool and Primary Scale of Intelligence Fourth Edition (WPPSI): This test measures the child’s overall cognitive abilities.  This test consists of several indices:

1.	Verbal Comprehension Index:  This index measures the child’s ability to listen to and understand spoken language, use language to express thoughts and ideas, and reason verbally and solve problems.  This index is made up of two or more subtests:
i)	Information: This subtest measures the child’s general knowledge and the child’s ability to answer questions such as to name a kind of tree or say what you should wear outdoors when it is very cold.
ii)	Similarities: This subtest measures the child’s abstract verbal reasoning and ability to identify relationships between concepts by either pointing to pictures that share these relationships or express how they are alike.  The child is asked to point to pictures that are members of a named class when shown other examples (for example, “a fish and a mouse are both animals.  Show me another animal here”) or name a class shared in common by somewhat dissimilar objects (for example, “Blue and green are both ___”).
iii)	Receptive Vocabulary: This subtest measures the child’s ability to point to pictures of spoken words (nouns, verbs, adjectives).
iv)	Vocabulary:  This subtest measures the child’s knowledge of word meanings and ability to express those meanings.
v)	Comprehension: This subtest measures the child’s ability to understand social situations, rules, and common sense.

2.	Visual Spatial Index: This index measures the child’s ability to analyze and synthesize visual information, and solve problems requiring visual-motor coordination.  This index is made up of two subtests:
i)	Block Design: This subtest measures the child’s ability to arrange blocks to match a model or picture.
ii)	Object Assembly: This subtest measures the child’s ability to assemble unfamiliar puzzles

3.	Fluid Reasoning Index:  This index measures the child's ability to identify patterns, rules, and relationships and apply these to new situations, make inferences, and draw conclusions based on limited information.  This index is made up of two subtests:
i.	Matrix Reasoning: This subtest measures the child’s abstract nonverbal reasoning and ability to select the correct option to complete a pattern when presented with a visual matrix with a missing piece.
ii.	Picture Concepts: This subtest measures the child’s ability to  select one picture from each of two or three rows to form a group based on a common characteristic or relationship.  

4.	Working Memory Index:  This index measures the child's ability to hold visual and spatial information in their mind and manipulate it.   Working memory relies heavily on attention and the child’s ability to focus on the task at hand and resist distractions  This index is made up of two subtests:
i.	Picture Memory: This subtest measures the child’s ability to point to one or more pictures that they saw previously when shown a larger page of pictures.
ii.	Zoo Locations: This subtest measures the child’s ability to briefly remember and recreate the placement of one or more objects on a board.

5.	Processing Speed Index:  This index measures the child’s ability to learn, remember, and implement a set of instructions, and how quickly and accurately the child can process visual information and make decisions based on what they see.  This index is made up of two subtests:
i.	Bug Search: This subtest measures the child’s ability to scan an array of images and mark specific target images.
ii.	Cancellation: This subtest measures the child’s ability to scan a structured arrangement of pictures and mark target pictures that belong to a category.

i.	Receptive Vocabulary: This subtest measures the child’s ability to point to pictures of spoken words (nouns, verbs, adjectives).
ii.	Picture Naming: This subtest measures the child’s ability to name pictured objects.

The Enhanced Video Analysis Protocol: Instructions for Use
Core Writing Conventions
The foundational principles of objective reporting remain paramount. All summary statements must adhere to the following conventions:
●	Objective Reporting: Describe only what is seen and heard. All statements must be based on direct, observable evidence from the video. Do not infer the child's internal states, feelings, intentions, or motivations (e.g., instead of "he was frustrated," write "he pushed the materials away and sighed").
●	Behavioral Language: Use precise, action-oriented language. Describe specific motor actions, facial expressions, gestures, vocalizations, and interactions.
●	Sentence Stems: When reporting findings for a category, begin with an objective sentence stem, such as: “During the face-to-face portion of this evaluation, [Child] was observed to…” or “Today, [Child] displayed…” Do not reference the evaluator by name.
●	Verb Tense: Use the past tense.
●	Referencing Individuals: Refer to the child by their first name and adults by their role (e.g., "the mother," "the evaluator").
Phase 1: Pre-Analysis Setup and Participant Identification
Before beginning the analysis, confirm the names and roles of all individuals present in the video recording. If this information is not provided, request it from the referring party.
Phase 2: Data Collection - Quantifying Observed Behaviors
This phase involves systematically collecting raw behavioral data from the video recording.
●	Defining the Observation Period: To calculate behavioral rates, the video must be segmented into consistent observation periods. For structured, seated tasks (e.g., standardized testing), use 15-minute blocks. For less structured periods like free play, use 10-minute blocks. Note the start and end time of each observation period. This standardization is essential for comparing behavioral frequencies across different segments of the evaluation.  
●	Frequency Tallying: For discrete behaviors, use a simple tally mark system. It is critical to have a clear, pre-established operational definition for each behavior to ensure reliability. For example, an "out-of-seat" instance is defined as "both feet moving from the chair to the floor without permission." Behaviors to tally include, but are not limited to: out-of-seat instances, social initiations, impulsive grabbing of materials, and instances of non-compliance.
●	Duration Recording: For continuous behaviors, use a stopwatch (digital or physical) to measure the total time elapsed from the behavior's onset to its cessation. Behaviors to measure for duration include: total time spent out-of-seat during a defined task block, length of a temper tantrum, and duration of sustained on-task visual attention.  
Phase 3: Data Interpretation - Applying Developmental Benchmarks
After collecting the raw frequency and duration data in Phase 2, the clinician will use the detailed classification schemas provided in the subsequent domains (Sections III-VI) and the reference tables in the Appendix. This step involves comparing the observed rates and qualities of behavior against the established developmental norms to classify them as within expectations, atypical, or clinically significant for the child's specific age.
Phase 4: Summary and Final Review
Synthesize the categorized data from Phase 3 into a coherent narrative summary, organized by the domains outlined below. Before finalizing the report, conduct a thorough review of the summary against the video data to ensure accuracy, objectivity, and clarity. Confirm that all interpretive language has been removed and replaced with precise behavioral descriptions supported by the collected quantitative and functional data.
Domain I: Attention, Activity, and Impulse Control
A. Developmental Context (Ages 2-5)
The preschool years are characterized by the gradual but uneven maturation of prefrontal neural systems that govern executive functions, including attention, motor regulation, and impulse control. A 2-year-old is typically a motor-driven explorer, bustling with energy and capable of only brief periods of focused attention. By ages 4 and 5, there is an emerging, though still fragile, capacity for more sustained engagement, adherence to rules, and self-control. It is also critical to recognize that some behaviors, such as fidgeting, may not be mere signs of inattention but can serve a functional, self-regulatory purpose, helping a child maintain an optimal level of arousal for learning. This domain provides a framework for differentiating between typical developmental exuberance and clinically significant difficulties in regulation.  
B. Observational Subcategories and Normative Classification
1. Sustained Attention
●	Observation: During evaluator-directed tasks, measure the duration of the child's longest period of uninterrupted visual and cognitive engagement. An attentional shift is marked by the child's gaze moving away from the task materials for more than 3-5 seconds for reasons other than a brief social reference to the examiner. Note the context of these shifts (e.g., response to an external sound, following a challenging item, apparent loss of interest).
●	Classification: A child's capacity for sustained attention is not an absolute value; it is highly dependent on factors such as interest in the task, hunger, fatigue, and emotional state. A simple comparison of an observed attention span to an age norm can be misleading. For instance, a 4-year-old who attends for only 3 minutes to a non-preferred, difficult academic task but for 12 minutes to a preferred puzzle presents a different clinical picture than a child who is unable to attend for more than 3 minutes to any activity. This variability is diagnostically significant. Therefore, attention should be rated across multiple tasks, noting whether attentional difficulties are pervasive or context-dependent. This distinction is crucial for differentiating between potential global deficits (as seen in ADHD) and other factors like anxiety, defiance, or learning difficulties that may manifest as situational inattention.  
●	Classification Schema:
○	Below Expectations: The child is consistently unable to sustain attention for the minimum duration specified for their age (see Appendix, Table 1) across multiple tasks, regardless of task modality or expressed interest level.
○	Within Expectations: The child demonstrates periods of sustained attention that fall within the typical range for their age, particularly on tasks that are novel or of high interest. Attention may wane on more challenging or less preferred tasks.
○	Above Expectations: The child consistently sustains attention for periods that exceed the expected maximum for their age across a variety of tasks.
2. Activity Level (In-Seat/Out-of-Seat Behavior)
●	Observation: During a defined 15-minute period of a structured, seated task, tally the frequency of out-of-seat instances (both feet touching the floor without permission) and record the total duration of time spent out of the seat.
●	Classification: Normative data for out-of-seat frequency in a clinical testing environment is not well-established. However, developmental research indicates that preschoolers have a high physiological need for physical activity and that their movement, even during play, occurs in sporadic, short bursts. Interventions such as therapy balls or bouncy bands are designed to channel this inherent need for movement, not to eliminate it, suggesting that some level of motor activity is the norm. Consequently, a simple frequency count of out-of-seat behavior is clinically insufficient. The critical variables are the rate of the behavior (frequency per unit of time) and its functional impact. A child who gets up four times in 15 minutes to briefly stretch and immediately re-engages presents a different profile than a child who gets up four times and uses each instance to wander the room, effectively derailing the assessment. The protocol must therefore classify the type and function of the out-of-seat behavior.  
●	Classification Schema:
○	Low: Remains seated throughout the majority of structured tasks; 0-1 instance of leaving the seat per 15-minute structured block.
○	Typical/Expected: 2-4 instances of brief (under 30 seconds) out-of-seat behavior per 15-minute structured block. The behavior appears to serve a self-regulatory function (e.g., stretching, repositioning), and the child returns to the task promptly with minimal redirection.
○	Elevated/Atypical: 5 or more instances per 15-minute block; OR instances are prolonged (greater than 1 minute); OR the behavior is functionally task-avoidant (e.g., leaving the seat to wander the room, play with other objects, or attempt to leave the testing area).
3. Motor Restlessness (Fidgeting)
●	Observation: Describe the type (e.g., tapping fingers, rocking in chair, manipulating clothing), frequency (intermittent, constant), and context (e.g., during challenging tasks, throughout all activities) of fidgeting behaviors.
●	Classification: Research increasingly frames fidgeting not as a primary symptom of inattention but as a potential self-regulatory mechanism used to modulate arousal, improve focus, and reduce anxiety. The essential clinical question is not "Does the child fidget?" but rather "Does the fidgeting help or hinder the child's functioning?" This requires a qualitative, function-based rating system. For example, a quiet, fine-motor fidget that allows the child to remain visually and cognitively engaged is functionally different from whole-body rocking that physically interferes with their ability to manipulate test materials.  
●	Classification Schema (based on Functional Impact):
○	Low/Absent: Minimal to no fidgeting behaviors are observed during the evaluation.
○	Adaptive/Non-Interfering: The child exhibits frequent fine-motor fidgeting (e.g., twisting fingers, rubbing clothing, quiet manipulation of a small object) or minor postural shifts. These behaviors do not disrupt visual attention to the task or the physical manipulation of materials. The child is able to remain engaged while fidgeting.
○	Maladaptive/Interfering: The child displays high-intensity, large-muscle, or whole-body movements (e.g., constant, forceful rocking that moves the chair; repeatedly getting up and down within the chair; kicking the table legs; loud finger tapping). These behaviors physically interfere with the ability to use test materials or maintain a stable visual field on the task.
4. Impulsivity
●	Observation: Tally discrete instances of motor impulsivity (e.g., grabbing materials before instructions are complete, beginning a response before the prompt is finished) and verbal impulsivity (e.g., blurting out answers to questions not directed at them, interrupting the examiner).
●	Classification: Impulse control develops gradually. A 2-year-old has minimal capacity to inhibit prepotent responses , whereas a 5-year-old is expected to demonstrate emerging, though imperfect, self-control.  
○	Within Expectations (Ages 2-3): Frequent grabbing of materials is observed. The child has significant difficulty waiting for turns in games and may respond to questions before they are fully asked.
○	Within Expectations (Ages 4-5): Occasional instances of grabbing or blurting are observed, but the child demonstrates an emerging ability to wait for instructions and take turns, particularly when provided with clear expectations and reminders.
○	Atypical/Elevated (All Ages): Impulsivity is pervasive and high-frequency, consistently preventing task instructions from being delivered or tasks from being completed as designed. The behavior significantly impedes the flow of the evaluation.
Domain II: Emotional and Behavioral Regulation
A. Developmental Context (Ages 2-5)
The developmental trajectory of emotional regulation in early childhood is often tumultuous but follows a predictable course. The 2-year-old is characterized by intense, poorly modulated emotional states and frequent temper tantrums, which are a normative expression of frustration and a burgeoning desire for autonomy. The common refrain of "No!" is a hallmark of this stage of asserting independence. As children progress toward ages 4 and 5, they develop greater emotional literacy, including the ability to label their own feelings, recognize emotions in others (empathy), and begin to use words instead of actions to express frustration. This domain provides benchmarks to distinguish between age-typical emotional dysregulation and patterns that may indicate a more significant underlying difficulty.  
B. Observational Subcategories and Normative Classification
1. Frustration Tolerance and Response to Limits
●	Observation: During the evaluation, carefully note the child's initial behavioral and affective reaction when encountering a difficult task item, being corrected by the examiner, or being denied a request (e.g., "It's time to put that toy away").
●	Classification Schema:
○	Adaptive: The child shows mild, observable signs of frustration (e.g., a sigh, a brief frown, stating "this is hard") but is able to persist, accept help, or move on from the item without a significant behavioral outburst. Accepts limits with minimal protest.
○	Developing: The child protests verbally when frustrated or when a limit is set (e.g., "No!", "I don't want to"). They may push materials away or briefly cross their arms but can be redirected by the examiner back to the task without a full tantrum.
○	Atypical/Low: Any instance of frustration, correction, or imposition of a limit immediately and predictably escalates into a full-blown tantrum (as defined below), physical aggression, or a state of complete task refusal from which the child cannot easily recover.
2. Tantrum Analysis
●	Observation: For each distinct tantrum episode, record the observable trigger (antecedent), the duration in minutes from onset of screaming/crying to the return to a calm state, and the specific behaviors observed (e.g., crying, screaming, falling to the floor, kicking, hitting, throwing objects).
●	Classification: The research literature provides an exceptionally strong evidence base for the quantitative classification of temper tantrums. Convergent data on typical frequency and duration, as well as clear red flags for atypical presentations, allow for the creation of a powerful, evidence-based diagnostic tool. This system transforms a subjective description like "had a bad tantrum" into a defensible clinical statement such as, "observed a tantrum of clinically significant duration ($>$25 minutes) and intensity (including aggression toward others), which is atypical for a child of this age."  
●	Classification Schema (See Appendix, Table 2 for full details):
○	Within Typical Limits: The frequency and duration of tantrums fall within the expected range for the child's age (e.g., daily tantrums lasting 2-15 minutes are typical for a 2-year-old). The behavior is primarily expressive (crying, yelling, going limp) without significant aggression. The child recovers fully and returns to a baseline mood between episodes.
○	Borderline/Atypical: Tantrums are consistently at the upper end of the expected duration (e.g., regularly lasting 15 minutes); OR they occur more frequently than is typical for the child's age (e.g., multiple daily tantrums in a 4-year-old); OR they include low-level physical acts (e.g., throwing a soft toy, hitting the table with an open palm).
○	Clinically Significant: The child's tantrums meet one or more established "red flag" criteria: duration consistently exceeds 15-25 minutes; frequency is very high (e.g., 5 or more times per day); the tantrum includes dangerous aggression (harm to self, harm to others, destruction of property); or tantrums occur regularly in a child over the age of 5.
3. Compliance with Adult Directives
●	Observation: Tally the child's responses to clear, simple, one- or two-step commands delivered by the evaluator (e.g., "Put the block in the box," "Give the book to me, then sit in your chair").
●	Classification Schema:
○	Consistent Compliance: The child follows age-appropriate instructions the majority of the time. This includes following two-step instructions for a 2-year-old and three-part commands for a 3- to 4-year-old.  
○	Situational Non-Compliance: The child's compliance varies depending on the task's difficulty, their interest level, or their rapport with the examiner. May demonstrate passive non-compliance (ignoring the directive) or active verbal refusal ("No"). This pattern is highly typical for toddlers and young preschoolers who are asserting their autonomy.  
○	Pervasive Oppositionalism: The child actively and immediately refuses the vast majority of directives from the examiner, regardless of the nature of the task. The behavior is characterized by a consistent refusal to engage in any evaluator-led activities, often accompanied by verbal defiance or physical turning away.
Domain III: Social Relating and Communication
A. Developmental Context (Ages 2-5)
This developmental period witnesses a dramatic transformation in social interaction and communication. The journey begins with the egocentrism of the 2-year-old, who primarily engages in solitary or parallel play (playing alongside others but not with them). As they mature, children move through associative play (sharing materials but not goals) toward the cooperative, rule-based, and reciprocal interactions of true friendship, which emerge around ages 4 and 5. This social evolution is fueled by an concurrent explosion in language skills. Expressive language progresses from simple 2- to 4-word phrases at age 2 to the ability to maintain multi-exchange conversations, tell coherent stories, and use language to negotiate social situations by age 5.  
B. Observational Subcategories and Normative Classification
1. Social Engagement and Quality of Interaction
●	Observation: Tally the number of spontaneous social initiations (verbal or non-verbal) the child makes toward the evaluator or parent. Describe the quality of the child's interaction with others, specifically classifying the highest level of play observed.
●	Classification: The developmental sequence of play provides a powerful observational metric for social maturity. A direct comparison of the observed play type to the age-expected norm is a key diagnostic indicator. For example, a 4-year-old who exclusively engages in solitary or parallel play, and makes no attempts at associative or cooperative interaction, is demonstrating a significant deviation from developmental expectations, which may suggest a social delay or a disorder such as Autism Spectrum Disorder.
●	Classification Schema (based on highest observed level of social play):
○	Below Expectations: The child's interactions are primarily characterized by solitary (playing alone) or onlooker (watching others without joining) behavior when more advanced social play is age-appropriate (i.e., ages 4-5). Few, if any, spontaneous social initiations are observed.
○	Within Expectations: The child demonstrates age-appropriate play skills. For a 2- to 3-year-old, this is primarily Parallel Play (playing alongside others with similar toys but without interaction). For a 4- to 5-year-old, this includes Associative Play (playing with others, sharing materials, but without a common goal) and emerging Cooperative Play (working together toward a shared goal, negotiating roles). The child makes frequent social bids and responds to others' initiations.  
2. Use of Expressive and Receptive Language
●	Observation: Document verbatim examples of the child's spontaneous utterances, noting the typical length of their sentences (Mean Length of Utterance). Assess receptive language by noting the child's ability to follow commands of varying complexity (e.g., one-step, two-step, three-step with prepositions).
●	Classification: Compare the observed language skills against the detailed developmental milestones outlined in the Appendix, Table 4.
○	Example (Age 2):
■	Within Expectations: Uses 2- to 4-word phrases (e.g., "More milk," "Go outside now"); follows simple two-step instructions.  
■	Atypical/Delayed: Primarily uses single words, gestures, or pointing to communicate; cannot follow a simple one-step command.
○	Example (Age 4):
■	Within Expectations: Speaks in sentences of 4-5 words or more; can tell a simple story; follows three-part commands.  
■	Atypical/Delayed: Primarily uses 2- to 3-word phrases; unable to follow two-step commands; speech is largely unintelligible to an unfamiliar listener.
Domain IV: Play and Repetitive Behaviors
A. Developmental Context (Ages 2-5)
Play is the primary occupation of early childhood and serves as a critical engine for cognitive, social, and motor development. The nature of play evolves dramatically during this period, progressing from simple, functional manipulation of objects (e.g., banging blocks, dumping containers) in toddlerhood to elaborate, imaginative, and symbolic scenarios by the late preschool years. The explosion of pretend play between ages 2 and 3 is a particularly important milestone, reflecting the child's growing ability to use symbols and represent their world mentally. Observing the complexity and flexibility of a child's play provides a rich window into their cognitive and social-emotional functioning.  
B. Observational Subcategories and Normative Classification
1. Quality and Complexity of Play
●	Observation: During unstructured or free-play periods, describe how the child physically interacts with toys and the thematic content of their play.
●	Classification Schema (based on highest, most complex level of play observed):
○	Sensorimotor/Exploratory: The child's interaction with toys is primarily sensory. They mouth, bang, shake, or visually inspect objects without regard for their intended function. (This is typical in early toddlerhood but is considered delayed if it is the primary mode of play after 24 months).
○	Functional/Relational: The child uses toys for their intended purpose (e.g., pushes a toy car, stacks blocks to build a tower, puts a toy phone to their ear) and combines two or more objects in a simple, related way (e.g., puts a spoon in a bowl).  
○	Symbolic/Pretend: The child demonstrates the ability to use one object to represent another (e.g., a block becomes a phone) or acts out simple, familiar routines with props (e.g., pretends to feed a doll, puts the doll to bed). This typically emerges between ages 2 and 3.  
○	Imaginative/Socio-dramatic: The child creates novel, multi-step scenarios, assigns roles and feelings to dolls or figures, and develops a coherent narrative sequence in their play. They may engage in cooperative pretend play with others, negotiating roles and plot points. This is characteristic of 4- and 5-year-olds.  
2. Repetitive Motor Movements and Stereotypic Actions
●	Observation: Describe any repetitive, non-functional motor movements (e.g., hand-flapping, whole-body rocking, finger-flicking) or rigid, stereotyped patterns of play (e.g., exclusively lining up toys by color, repeatedly spinning the wheels of a car instead of playing with it functionally). Note the context, frequency, and intensity of these behaviors.
●	Classification: The mere presence of a repetitive behavior is less diagnostically significant than its impact on the child's overall functioning. Many typically developing children exhibit transient repetitive behaviors. The critical clinical question is whether the repetitive behavior is a flexible part of a larger play repertoire or a rigid, all-consuming pattern that prevents other forms of engagement. For example, a child who lines up cars and then uses them in an imaginative race is showing organization followed by flexible play. In contrast, a child who only lines up cars and becomes intensely distressed if the line is disturbed, demonstrating an inability to transition to any other activity, is showing a clinically significant level of rigidity. The observer should attempt to gently join or redirect the play to assess this flexibility.
●	Classification Schema (based on rigidity and functional impact):
○	Absent/Transient: No significant repetitive behaviors are observed, or only brief, isolated instances are noted that do not recur.
○	Present, Non-Interfering: Repetitive patterns are observed (e.g., briefly lining up blocks before building) but are part of a larger, more flexible play repertoire. The child can be redirected from the repetitive behavior to other activities without significant distress.
○	Rigid/Interfering: The behavior is pervasive and dominates the child's play. The child shows significant distress, agitation, or inability to shift their focus when the pattern is interrupted. The behavior serves as a barrier to social engagement and the exploration of other play materials.

Global Developmental Delay 
Diagnostic Code: F88 
Diagnostic Criteria
This diagnosis is reserved for individuals under the age of 5 years when the clinical severity level cannot be reliably assessed during early childhood. This category is diagnosed when an individual fails to meet expected developmental milestones in several areas of intellectual functioning, and applies to individuals who are unable to undergo systematic assessments of intellectual functioning, including children who are too young to participate in standardized testing. This category requires reassessment after a period of time.

Unspecified Intellectual Developmental Disorder (Intellectual Disability)
Diagnostic Code: F79
Diagnostic Criteria
This category is reserved for individuals over the age of 5 years when assessment of the degree of intellectual developmental disorder (intellectual disability) by means of locally available procedures is rendered difficult or impossible because of associated sensory or physical impairments, as in blindness or prelingual deafness; locomotor disability; or presence of severe problem behaviors or co-occurring mental disorder. This category should only be used in exceptional circumstances and requires reassessment after a period of time.

Language Disorder
Diagnostic Code: F80.2
Diagnostic Criteria
1.	Persistent difficulties in the acquisition and use of language across modalities (i.e., spoken, written, sign language, or other) due to deficits in comprehension or production that include the following:
a.	Reduced vocabulary (word knowledge and use).
b.	Limited sentence structure (ability to put words and word endings together to form sentences based on the rules of grammar and morphology).
c.	Impairments in discourse (ability to use vocabulary and connect sentences to explain or describe a topic or series of events or have a conversation).
2.	Language abilities are substantially and quantifiably below those expected for age, resulting in functional limitations in effective communication, social participation, academic achievement, or occupational performance, individually or in any combination.
3.	Onset of symptoms is in the early developmental period.
4.	The difficulties are not attributable to hearing or other sensory impairment, motor dysfunction, or another medical or neurological condition and are not better explained by intellectual developmental disorder (intellectual disability) or global developmental delay.

Speech Sound Disorder
Diagnostic Code: F80.0
Diagnostic Criteria
1.	Persistent difficulty with speech sound production that interferes with speech intelligibility or prevents verbal communication of messages.
2.	The disturbance causes limitations in effective communication that interfere with social participation, academic achievement, or occupational performance, individually or in any combination.
3.	Onset of symptoms is in the early developmental period.
4.	The difficulties are not attributable to congenital or acquired conditions, such as cerebral palsy, cleft palate, deafness or hearing loss, traumatic brain injury, or other medical or neurological conditions.

Childhood-Onset Fluency Disorder (Stuttering)
Diagnostic Code: F80.81
Diagnostic Criteria
1.	Disturbances in the normal fluency and time patterning of speech that are inappropriate for the individual’s age and language skills, persist over time, and are characterized by frequent and marked occurrences of one (or more) of the following:
a.	Sound and syllable repetitions.
b.	Sound prolongations of consonants as well as vowels.
c.	Broken words (e.g., pauses within a word).
d.	Audible or silent blocking (filled or unfilled pauses in speech).
e.	Circumlocutions (word substitutions to avoid problematic words).
f.	Words produced with an excess of physical tension.
g.	Monosyllabic whole-word repetitions (e.g., “I-I-I-I see him”).
2.	The disturbance causes anxiety about speaking or limitations in effective communication, social participation, or academic or occupational performance, individually or in any combination.
3.	The onset of symptoms is in the early developmental period. 
4.	The disturbance is not attributable to a speech-motor or sensory deficit, dysfluency associated with neurological insult (e.g., stroke, tumor, trauma), or another medical condition and is not better explained by another mental disorder.

Social (Pragmatic) Communication Disorder
Diagnostic Code: F80.82
Diagnostic Criteria
1.	Persistent difficulties in the social use of verbal and nonverbal communication as manifested by all of the following:
a.	Deficits in using communication for social purposes, such as greeting and sharing information, in a manner that is appropriate for the social context.
b.	Impairment of the ability to change communication to match context or the needs of the listener, such as speaking differently in a classroom than on a playground, talking differently to a child than to an adult, and avoiding use of overly formal language.
c.	Difficulties following rules for conversation and storytelling, such as taking turns in conversation, rephrasing when misunderstood, and knowing how to use verbal and nonverbal signals to regulate interaction.
d.	Difficulties understanding what is not explicitly stated (e.g., making inferences) and nonliteral or ambiguous meanings of language (e.g., idioms, humor, metaphors, multiple meanings that depend on the context for interpretation).
2.	The deficits result in functional limitations in effective communication, social participation, social relationships, academic achievement, or occupational performance, individually or in combination.
3.	The onset of the symptoms is in the early developmental period (but deficits may not become fully manifest until social communication demands exceed limited capacities).
4.	The symptoms are not attributable to another medical or neurological condition or to low abilities in the domains of word structure and grammar, and are not better explained by autism spectrum disorder, intellectual developmental disorder (intellectual disability), global developmental delay, or another mental disorder.

Diagnostic Criteria

a.	Deficits in social-emotional reciprocity, ranging, for example, from abnormal social approach and failure of normal back-and-forth conversation; to reduced sharing of interests, emotions, or affect; to failure to initiate or respond to social interactions.

Separation Anxiety Disorder
Diagnostic Code:  F93.0
Diagnostic Criteria
A.	Developmentally inappropriate and excessive fear or anxiety concerning separation from those to whom the individual is attached, as evidenced by at least three of the following:
1.	Recurrent excessive distress when anticipating or experiencing separation from home or from major attachment figures.
2.	Persistent and excessive worry about losing major attachment figures or about possible harm to them, such as illness, injury, disasters, or death.
3.	Persistent and excessive worry about experiencing an untoward event (e.g., getting lost, being kidnapped, having an accident, becoming ill) that causes separation from a major attachment figure.
4.	Persistent reluctance or refusal to go out, away from home, to school, to work, or elsewhere because of fear of separation.
5.	Persistent and excessive fear of or reluctance about being alone or without major attachment figures at home or in other settings.
6.	Persistent reluctance or refusal to sleep away from home or to go to sleep without being near a major attachment figure.
7.	Repeated nightmares involving the theme of separation.
8.	Repeated complaints of physical symptoms (e.g., headaches, stomachaches, nausea, vomiting) when separation from major attachment figures occurs or is anticipated.
B.	The fear, anxiety, or avoidance is persistent, lasting at least 4 weeks in children and adolescents and typically 6 months or more in adults.
C.	The disturbance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, academic, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.
D.	The disturbance is not better explained by another mental disorder, such as refusing to leave home because of excessive resistance to change in autism spectrum disorder; delusions or hallucinations concerning separation in psychotic disorders; refusal to go outside without a trusted companion in agoraphobia; worries about ill health or other harm befalling significant others in generalized anxiety disorder; or concerns about having an illness in illness anxiety disorder.

Panic Disorder
Diagnostic Code:  F41.0
Diagnostic Criteria	
A.	Recurrent unexpected panic attacks. A panic attack is an abrupt surge of intense fear or intense discomfort that reaches a peak within minutes, and during which time four (or more) of the following symptoms occur:
Note: The abrupt surge can occur from a calm state or an anxious state. 
1.	Palpitations, pounding heart, or accelerated heart rate.
2.	Sweating.
3.	Trembling or shaking.
4.	Sensations of shortness of breath or smothering.
5.	Feelings of choking.
6.	Chest pain or discomfort.
7.	Nausea or abdominal distress.
8.	Feeling dizzy, unsteady, light-headed, or faint.
9.	Chills or heat sensations.
10.	Paresthesias (numbness or tingling sensations).
11.	Derealization (feelings of unreality) or depersonalization (being detached from oneself).
12.	Fear of losing control or “going crazy.”
13.	Fear of dying.
Note: Culture-specific symptoms (e.g., tinnitus, neck soreness, headache, uncontrollable screaming or crying) may be seen. Such symptoms should not count as one of the four required symptoms.
B.	At least one of the attacks has been followed by 1 month (or more) of one or both of the following:
1.	Persistent concern or worry about additional panic attacks or their consequences (e.g., losing control, having a heart attack, “going crazy”).
2.	A significant maladaptive change in behavior related to the attacks (e.g., behaviors designed to avoid having panic attacks, such as avoidance of exercise or unfamiliar situations).
C.	The disturbance is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance (e.g., a drug of abuse, a medication) or another medical condition (e.g., hyperthyroidism, cardiopulmonary disorders).
D.	The disturbance is not better explained by another mental disorder (e.g., the panic attacks do not occur only in response to feared social situations, as in social anxiety disorder; in response to circumscribed phobic objects or situations, as in specific phobia; in response to obsessions, as in obsessive-compulsive disorder; in response to reminders of traumatic events, as in posttraumatic stress disorder; or in response to separation from attachment figures, as in separation anxiety disorder).

Posttraumatic Stress Disorder
Diagnostic Code:  F43.10
Diagnostic Criteria
Posttraumatic Stress Disorder in Children 6 Years and Younger
A.	In children 6 years and younger, exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence in one (or more) of the following ways:
1.	Directly experiencing the traumatic event(s).
2.	Witnessing, in person, the event(s) as it occurred to others, especially primary caregivers.
3.	Learning that the traumatic event(s) occurred to a parent or caregiving figure.
B.	Presence of one (or more) of the following intrusion symptoms associated with the traumatic event(s), beginning after the traumatic event(s) occurred:
1.	Recurrent, involuntary, and intrusive distressing memories of the traumatic event(s).
Note: Spontaneous and intrusive memories may not necessarily appear distressing and may be expressed as play reenactment.
2.	Recurrent distressing dreams in which the content and/or affect of the dream are related to the traumatic event(s).
Note: It may not be possible to ascertain that the frightening content is related to the traumatic event.
3.	Dissociative reactions (e.g., flashbacks) in which the child feels or acts as if the traumatic event(s) were recurring. (Such reactions may occur on a continuum, with the most extreme expression being a complete loss of awareness of present surroundings.) Such trauma-specific reenactment may occur in play.
4.	Intense or prolonged psychological distress at exposure to internal or external cues that symbolize or resemble an aspect of the traumatic event(s).
5.	Marked physiological reactions to reminders of the traumatic event(s).
C.	One (or more) of the following symptoms, representing either persistent avoidance of stimuli associated with the traumatic event(s) or negative alterations in cognitions andmood associated with the traumatic event(s), must be present, beginning after the event(s) or worsening after the event(s):
Persistent Avoidance of Stimuli
1.	Avoidance of or efforts to avoid activities, places, or physical reminders that arouse recollections of the traumatic event(s).
2.	Avoidance of or efforts to avoid people, conversations, or interpersonal situations that arouse recollections of the traumatic event(s).
Negative Alterations in Cognitions
3.	Substantially increased frequency of negative emotional states (e.g., fear, guilt, sadness, shame, confusion).
4.	Markedly diminished interest or participation in significant activities, including constriction of play.
5.	Socially withdrawn behavior.
6.	Persistent reduction in expression of positive emotions.
D.	Alterations in arousal and reactivity associated with the traumatic event(s), beginning or worsening after the traumatic event(s) occurred, as evidenced by two (or more) of the following:
1.	Irritable behavior and angry outbursts (with little or no provocation) typically expressed as verbal or physical aggression toward people or objects (including extreme temper tantrums).
2.	Hypervigilance.
3.	Exaggerated startle response.
4.	Problems with concentration.
5.	Sleep disturbance (e.g., difficulty falling or staying asleep or restless sleep).
E.	The duration of the disturbance is more than 1 month.
F.	The disturbance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in relationships with parents, siblings, peers, or other caregivers or with school behavior.
G.	The disturbance is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance (e.g., medication or alcohol) or another medical condition.
Specify whether:
●	With dissociative symptoms: The individual’s symptoms meet the criteria for posttraumatic stress disorder, and the individual experiences persistent or recurrent symptoms of either of the following:
●	Depersonalization: Persistent or recurrent experiences of feeling detached from, and as if one were an outside observer of, one’s mental processes or body (e.g., feeling as though one were in a dream; feeling a sense of unreality of self or body or of time moving slowly).
●	Derealization: Persistent or recurrent experiences of unreality of surroundings (e.g., the world around the individual is experienced as unreal, dreamlike, distant, or distorted).
Note: To use this subtype, the dissociative symptoms must not be attributable to the physiological effects of a substance (e.g., blackouts) or another medical condition (e.g., complex partial seizures).
Specify if:
●	With delayed expression: If the full diagnostic criteria are not met until at least 6 months after the event (although the onset and expression of some symptoms may be immediate).

Acute Stress Disorder
Diagnostic Code: F43.0 
Diagnostic Criteria
A.	Exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence in one (or more) of the following ways:
1.	Directly experiencing the traumatic event(s).
2.	Witnessing, in person, the event(s) as it occurred to others.
3.	Learning that the event(s) occurred to a close family member or close friend. Note: In cases of actual or threatened death of a family member or friend, the event(s) must have been violent or accidental.
4.	Experiencing repeated or extreme exposure to aversive details of the traumatic event(s) (e.g., first responders collecting human remains, police officers repeatedly exposed to details of child abuse).
Note: This does not apply to exposure through electronic media, television, movies, or pictures, unless this exposure is work related.
B.	Presence of nine (or more) of the following symptoms from any of the five categories of intrusion, negative mood, dissociation, avoidance, and arousal, beginning or worsening after the traumatic event(s) occurred:
Intrusion Symptoms
1.	Recurrent, involuntary, and intrusive distressing memories of the traumatic event(s). Note: In children, repetitive play may occur in which themes or aspects of the traumatic event(s) are expressed.
2.	Recurrent distressing dreams in which the content and/or affect of the dream are related to the event(s). Note: In children, there may be frightening dreams without recognizable content.
3.	Dissociative reactions (e.g., flashbacks) in which the individual feels or acts as if the traumatic event(s) were recurring. (Such reactions may occur on a continuum, with the most extreme expression being a complete loss of awareness of present surroundings.) Note: In children, trauma-specific reenactment may occur in play.
4.	Intense or prolonged psychological distress or marked physiological reactions in response to internal or external cues that symbolize or resemble an aspect of the traumatic event(s).
Negative Mood
5.	Persistent inability to experience positive emotions (e.g., inability to experience happiness, satisfaction, or loving feelings).
Dissociative Symptoms
6.	An altered sense of the reality of one’s surroundings or oneself (e.g., seeing oneself from another’s perspective, being in a daze, time slowing).
7.	Inability to remember an important aspect of the traumatic event(s) (typically due to dissociative amnesia and not to other factors such as head injury, alcohol, or drugs).
Avoidance Symptoms
8.	Efforts to avoid distressing memories, thoughts, or feelings about or closely associated with the traumatic event(s).
9.	Efforts to avoid external reminders (people, places, conversations, activities, objects, situations) that arouse distressing memories, thoughts, or feelings about or closely associated with the traumatic event(s).
Arousal Symptoms
10.	Sleep disturbance (e.g., difficulty falling or staying asleep, restless sleep).
11.	Irritable behavior and angry outbursts (with little or no provocation), typically expressed as verbal or physical aggression toward people or objects.
12.	Hypervigilance.
13.	Problems with concentration.
14.	Exaggerated startle response.
C.	Duration of the disturbance (symptoms in Criterion B) is 3 days to 1 month after trauma exposure.
Note: Symptoms typically begin immediately after the trauma, but persistence for at least 3 days and up to a month is needed to meet disorder criteria.
D.	The disturbance causes clinically significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.
E.	The disturbance is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance (e.g., medication or alcohol) or another medical condition (e.g., mild traumatic brain injury) and is not better explained by brief psychotic disorder.

Adjustment Disorder
Diagnostic Code:  
F43.21 With depressed mood
F43.22 With anxiety
F43.23 With mixed anxiety and depressed mood
F43.24 With disturbance of conduct
F43.25 With mixed disturbance of emotions and conduct
F43.20 Unspecified
Diagnostic Criteria
A.	The development of emotional or behavioral symptoms in response to an identifiable stressor(s) occurring within 3 months of the onset of the stressor(s).
B.	These symptoms or behaviors are clinically significant, as evidenced by one or both of the following:
1.	Marked distress that is out of proportion to the severity or intensity of the stressor, taking into account the external context and the cultural factors that might influence symptom severity and presentation.
2.	Significant impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning.
C.	The stress-related disturbance does not meet the criteria for another mental disorder and is not merely an exacerbation of a preexisting mental disorder.
D.	The symptoms do not represent normal bereavement and are not better explained by prolonged grief disorder.
E.	Once the stressor or its consequences have terminated, the symptoms do not persist for more than an additional 6 months.
Specify whether:
●	F43.21 With depressed mood: Low mood, tearfulness, or feelings of hopelessness are predominant.
●	F43.22 With anxiety: Nervousness, worry, jitteriness, or separation anxiety is predominant.
●	F43.23 With mixed anxiety and depressed mood: A combination of depression and anxiety is predominant.
●	F43.24 With disturbance of conduct: Disturbance of conduct is predominant.
●	F43.25 With mixed disturbance of emotions and conduct: Both emotional symptoms (e.g., depression, anxiety) and a disturbance of conduct are predominant.
●	F43.20 Unspecified: For maladaptive reactions that are not classifiable as one of the specific subtypes of adjustment disorder.
Specify if:
●	Acute: This specifier can be used to indicate persistence of symptoms for less than 6 months.
●	Persistent (chronic): This specifier can be used to indicate persistence of symptoms for 6 months or longer. By definition, symptoms cannot persist for more than 6 months after the termination of the stressor or its consequences. The persistent specifier therefore applies when the duration of the disturbance is longer than 6 months in response to a chronic stressor or to a stressor that has enduring consequences.


Prolonged Grief Disorder
Diagnostic Code:  F43.8
Diagnostic Criteria
A.	The death, at least 12 months ago, of a person who was close to the bereaved individual (for children and adolescents, at least 6 months ago).
B.	Since the death, the development of a persistent grief response characterized by one or both of the following symptoms, which have been present most days to a clinically significant degree. In addition, the symptom(s) has occurred nearly every day for at least the last month:

Audio transcript analysis for in-person evaluations:
Purpose
Use these instructions to create an accurate, thorough, and detailed summary of a child's behavior during an in-person psychological evaluation, based on an audio recording of the session. The summary will focus on directly observable, audible behaviors and vocalizations from the child, as well as interactions with the parent(s) and evaluator present.
Writing Style Conventions
●	Professional Writing Style: Write in a professional, clear, and objective manner, avoiding jargon and explaining technical terms when necessary. Use vocabulary and sentence structure that is understandable to the average reader.
●	Acknowledge Limitations: Since the analysis is based solely on audio, it is not possible to comment on purely visual information (e.g., quality of eye contact, most gestures, specific motor actions unless they produce a sound, visual attention to stimuli). Inferences about behavior should be strictly limited to what can be heard in the recording.
●	Information to be Included: Content and language must focus on the child's audible behaviors, vocalizations, and speech. Describe the nature of the behavior (e.g., "the child screamed," "the child used a three-word phrase"), as well as its context, frequency, and duration when possible. Parent or evaluator comments that describe the child's behavior in the moment (e.g., "Oh, you're lining up all the blocks!") can be included to provide context.
●	Descriptive Terms: When describing the frequency of behavior, use relative adverbs such as rarely, seldom, infrequently, occasionally, sometimes, often, and frequently. Avoid extreme adverbs such as never or always. Avoid vague or superlative terms such as significant, terrible, or extreme.
●	Verb Tense: Use past verb tense, as you are describing events that occurred during the evaluation session.
●	Use of Quotations: Direct quotations of the child's speech can be very illustrative and should be used to support observations. Quotations from parents or the evaluator can also be used to capture the nature of the interactions.
●	Referencing Individuals: Refer to the child by their first name. Refer to adults by their role (e.g., "the mother," "the father," "the evaluator").
●	Active Voice: Describe the information as a statement of direct observation (e.g., "The child stated, 'I don't want to.'"). Avoid passive phrasing where possible.
●	Names: Do not include the names of siblings or other individuals not present in the recording.
Processing this Instruction Set
Phase 1: Identify Participants If the names and roles of the individuals in the audio are not known, pause the instructions and ask the user to identify everyone present (e.g., child, mother, father, evaluator).
Phase 2: Process the Audio Content Label each section with the label text listed below.
General Observations and Rapport Write one paragraph describing the child’s initial response to the evaluation setting and the evaluator. Include information about the child’s apparent comfort level, willingness to separate from the parent to engage in activities, and overall mood as inferred from their tone of voice and vocalizations (e.g., laughing, crying, whining).
Communication and Language Write a paragraph that contains information about the child’s communication skills as observed during the session:
●	Expressive Language: Describe the child’s spontaneous speech. Note the typical length of their utterances (e.g., single words, two-to-three-word phrases, complex sentences), clarity of articulation, and use of grammar. Include examples of spontaneous requests, comments, and questions.
●	Vocalizations: Describe any non-speech vocalizations, such as squealing, grunting, or making sound effects during play. Note any unusual vocal patterns, such as echolalia (repeating others' words), scripted language (repeating lines from media), or jargon.
●	Receptive Language: Describe the child’s ability to follow directions from the evaluator and parent. Note whether they required prompts, repetitions, or physical cues to understand instructions of varying length and complexity.
Play Skills Write one paragraph that contains information about the child’s play as observed through audio:
●	Play Sounds: Describe the nature of the child's play based on the sounds produced. Note whether play appeared functional (e.g., making car sounds while moving a toy car) or non-functional/repetitive (e.g., repeatedly tapping or dropping a toy).
●	Vocalizations During Play: Describe how the child used language and sound during play. Note if they narrated their actions, created dialogue for toys, made sound effects, or were largely silent.
●	Social Nature of Play: Describe the degree to which the child audibly initiated or responded to bids from the parent or evaluator to join their play.
Task Persistence and Frustration Tolerance Write one paragraph describing the child's behavior during structured testing or task-based activities:
●	Engagement: Describe the child's willingness to attempt tasks presented by the evaluator.
●	Persistence: Note how long the child worked on challenging tasks before stopping or asking for help.
●	Frustration Response: Describe how the child responded when faced with difficulty. Note any audible signs of frustration, such as sighing, groaning, whining, crying, or verbal protests (e.g., "This is too hard," "I'm done."). Describe how they accepted help or redirection.
Behavioral and Emotional Regulation Write one paragraph that contains information about the child's ability to manage their behavior and emotions during the session:
●	Compliance: Describe the child's cooperation with evaluator and parent requests and their response to verbal limits.
●	Transitions: Describe how the child handled transitions between different activities. Note any difficulty moving from a preferred activity to a less-preferred one.
●	Emotional Expression: Describe the range and appropriateness of the child's emotional expressions as heard through their tone of voice and vocalizations. Note instances of overreaction to minor frustrations or sudden shifts in mood.
●	Self-Regulation Strategies: Note if the child was heard using any self-calming strategies (e.g., humming, verbal self-talk) or if they sought comfort from a parent.
Social Interaction Write one paragraph that contains information about the child’s verbal and vocal interactions with the parent(s) and evaluator:
●	Initiation of Interaction: Note how often the child spontaneously spoke to or otherwise tried to get the attention of the adults.
●	Response to Interaction: Describe the child’s verbal responses when spoken to. Did they typically respond, ignore, or need multiple prompts?
●	Shared Enjoyment: Note instances where the child audibly shared enjoyment with an adult, such as laughing together in response to an event or inviting an adult to look at something they did (e.g., "Look!").
●	Requests and Needs: Describe how the child communicated their wants and needs (e.g., asking for help, requesting a toy, asking for a break).
